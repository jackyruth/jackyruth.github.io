Magnetic Robotic Car

Using custom transceiver, reciever circuits to control a car through the magnetic field

Engineering lab course | April, 2018

 

The goal of this project is to construct a minimalistic car with 5 instructions: forwards, backwards, left, right and stop. These instructions must be communicated using a transmitter and a receiver circuit. I made the transmitter and receiver circuit while the rest of the team worked on the car.

The transmitter circuit is a simple RLC series circuit that is being driven with a H bridge at the RLC circuit's oscillation frequency. In figure 1, the circuit in focus is the transmitter circuit. You can see the H bridge directly below and to the left of the massive inductor-capacitor pair. You can also see two BJTs responsible for operating the gates of the MOSFETs. The BJTs are driven by the microcontroller shown on the left.

Figure 1: Transmitter Circuit

It took some simple calculations to find the optimal capacitance value which was 0.047 uF capacitor. Along with the 1mH inductor, the voltage produced across the inductor reached 800 volts peak to peak (with a 9 volt power supply), drew 1.4 amps and oscillated at 21.8 kHz.

 

The receiver circuit was considerably more difficult to design. In figure 2, I drew out the main components of the receiver circuit. Going from left to right, the receiver circuit is composed of a tank circuit which feeds its output into two amplifiers cascaded together. The amplified signal is then sent through a simple diode rectifier whose output is then compared to 2.5 volts with a comparator. The output of the comparator would be 1 if there is a magnetic field and 0 if there is not.

Figure 2: Receiver Circuit Schematic

Besides the main design, several peripheral components were added such as a high pass filter to remove any dc component coming from the tank circuit and extra capacitors to further separate the power and ground lines. We also fed the output through a NOT gate because the signal being sent is a NOT-ed version of the signal we want. This is because of the UART communication protocol keeps the signal output high even when nothing is being sent to signal that the communication channel is not broken. We do not want this because the inductor heats up and increases its resistance when its sending a high signal. The NOT gates are there to keep the inductor cool.

Figure 3: (Left) Receiver circuit, (right) motor driver for the car

After the transmission quality is to my satisfaction, the team integrated their hacked, NES controller with my transmitter circuit.

Figure 4: Controlling the Car with an NES controller

 

An issue that came up was the loose connection the motor's multithread wires had to the breadboard. We made the connections robust by soldering the multithread wires to solid core wires. Then we put it all together.

Figure 5: Completed Car

The car was responsive to commands, it executes all 5 instructions flawlessly and it has the longest range (3.5 meters) of all the cars in my year. We obtained 95% on this project.
