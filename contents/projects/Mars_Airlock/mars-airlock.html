Airlock Project

Martian Airlock constructed by the UBC Mars Colony Team

Engineering Student Team | September 2017 -- June 2019

 

 

A variety of sensor data is important in an Airlock for safety reasons. There must be CO2 sensor, O2 sensor, temperature sensor and pressure sensor. The picture above is that of an electrochemical CO2 sensor. It would not work on mars, but mars-grade electronics would not be feasible as they do not exist/ or is very expensive.

 

 

Shown below is our sensor box. It communicates to a central computer which is a raspberry pi. As you can, the CO2 sensor is in our sensor box along with an O2 sensor, a temperature sensor and a pressure sensor that can measure up to 2 atms. The sensor box is the first electrical box we finished working on.

The following electrical box is the power module for our airlock. I made this box completely by myself. It is essentially 6 power regulators which has a 24 VDC input and a 3.3, 5, 10, 14 and 24 VDC output. The metallic box to the left of the picture is a commercial power supply that takes in 110 VAC from the wall socket and converts it to 24 VDC. The electronic system of our Airlock runs on wall socket power.

 

Shown below is our pressure system. It is simply two high voltage switchers sitting on top of an Arduino. The additional circuitry allows the low voltage pin of the Arduino to switch 12 volt pressure valves.

 

 

This is our input controller box, easily the most complicated of our electrical boxes in terms of software and hardware. It has got a lot of push buttons, 7 segment displays, switches, display LEDs and an emergency stop switch.

 

 

As you can see, the input box houses the main computer, the raspberry pi and an Arduino to run the 7 segment displays

 

There are several electrical components in this picture. There is the motor controller box, which houses an Arduino Nano and a Stepper Motor Driver. That driver is, of course, connected to a stepper motor that is off screen. There is another high voltage switcher that switches the door strike, which locks the door when it closes. Another component not seen here is the contact switch, which provides feedback as to where the door is located to the Nano.

 

All the boxes above are housed inside the airlock. But there requires a box located outside of the airlock which the astronaut need to get into the airlock. This is the box. It has 2 buttons, an emergency stop pushbutton and a 7seg display featuring the most important sensor data, that of the pressure inside the airlock.

 

 

Of course, it is hard to see in an closed airlock without lights. This fixes that problem, the circuit is also essentially a high voltage switcher. It switches 110 VAC with 5 VDC. Now that I think about it, a lot of the hardware is just switching different, higher voltages with lower voltages.
