On August 17th, 2019, I rewatched 2001: a space odyssey in UHD. It was more phenomenal than I remember, the tense atmosphere Stanley Kubrick crafted has never since been replicated.

 

My favorite parts of the movie has shifted from the stunning visuals of the planets and the ever mystifying monolith to the astronauts, how they conduct themselves and how they work, and how deliberate they are in their words and their actions during tense situations.

 

That is why I opted to have the Extravehicular Activity pod (EVA pod) version of the poster because it somehow signifies the stoic, and the focused, get-down-to-work attitude which I so admire in David Bowman and Francis Poole.

 

The movie was shot in a very realistic fashion. There was only 600 or so shots in this 2.5 hr long movie, which allowed one to admire the extreme detail and the slow, careful deployment of technology. I think the amount of time the movie allows the scene to settle allowed me to treat the astronauts as real people and observe how they conduct themselves in their long voyage to Jupiter.

 

On a casual glance, both the astronaut doesn't seem to do much of anything at all. Frank Poole slowly eat his food as he watches the interview he conducted. David Bowman sketches, do some tame exercises and plays chess with HAL 9000. Granted that they were on a long isolated journey to Jupiter, but the slowness of their action lends a sense of peace, similar to the feeling of the dull hours on a international flight. I am quite attracted to the idea of focused, slow relaxation, particularly since these astronauts proved that they are highly intelligent men, seemingly without ego, and ready to be deadly-focused and get down to work at the moments notice.

 

This is what I want to strive for, this lazing Captain-Picard reading a book type of moments, filling the time with focused relaxation and casual knowledge acquisition. An immense, but still very much human, intellect ready to focus and get stuff done.

 

The investigation on deep work skills (which includes how to relax properly) aims to achieve this ideal.

 

 

Update 2020-04-18

 

I have been working on Verdi, the agricultural technology startup of which I am the hardware co-founder. I laden myself with the massive workload of designing the hardware for Verdi's two pilot projects at Arterra and Quail's Gate. There was so much to do all the time, and in the beginning, I had a tendency to move as fast I possible. It is only 3 months later, after I had gone through some of the consequences of my moving-as-fast as I possibly could, that I realized there laid a better path from January to April, than the one I had taken.

 

In matters of engineering, and I think problem solving in general, always work with intent, focus and diligence. Whether you are tinkering with hardware, exploring with programming and mathematics or performing actions involved in solving a problem or resolving a situation, keep a clear description of the action taken. Observe closely, the resulting affect of that action and hold both the action and the observation present in your mind as you inch forward towards the solution. Do not let timelines and the long list of to-do tasks perturb your mind from engaging in this cognitively demanding fashion of problem solving.

 

I realized that had I "moved less fast" and been more engaged in mentally recording my actions and its effect on the hardware, I wouldn't have wasted so much time and resources recreating bugs. I could have skipped the "recreate and record bug" stage and moved on directly to the debugging stage. This would have definitely saved me a lot of time and money in the last three months.

 

In realizing this, two things clicked in my mind. This better way of problem solving is manifested in Astronauts in 2001 and the officers in Star Trek TNG. When one watches these films, one can clearly see that the scenes emphasizes that the characters employ a cause-and-effect approach to problem solving. In every situation, even life and death, the character of David Bowman always takes the time to carefully consider his actions, and when he has made his decision, do them. Upon completing his action, he always pauses and observes the changes that results, before considering his next action again. I shall name this problem-solving mindset the David Bowman technique.

 

The difficulty in adhering to the David Bowman technique is that whenever a task is successfully completed, one gets the feeling that it would have been completely successfully even without the rigorous attention to detail. Eventually, perhaps due to the success of the David Bowman technique, one feels as if things can be done much faster if more careless heuristics were to be used. The success of the David Bowman technique would also be the cause of its downfall from grace. On the other hand, if the task is completed successfully with someone employing the wishy washy, do-everything-as-fast-as-possible approach, one gets a sense of achievement and pride that comes with completing a task so quickly. And purely due to the nature of projects, many more tasks are completely successfully than are not, the feedback is not on the David Bowman technique's side, even if the few unsuccessful tasks turn out to be the biggest expenditure of time.

 

But having gone through the last three months, I know better. I will train to employ the David Bowman technique.

 

The David Bowman technique becomes more powerful when accompanied by another problem-solving (project-making) principle I call the principle of least action. This principle says to only do what is absolutely necessary to achieve one's goal. This principle is the greatest time saver of all. Ask yourself, is this task that I am about to embark on absolutely necessary for the success of the project ( or the solution of the problem ). If not, then find something that is and focus on that.

 

Here is an example. About 3 weeks before the deployment of the hardware devices, I started to consider how I can characterize those devices. I told myself that by knowing more about the devices, then if there was an issue with a device, I can debug it and fix it much faster. But 3 weeks before deployment is exactly the time where a lot of devices need to be assembled and verified. I asked myself, what does the affect of characterizing the devices have on the assembly and deployment of those devices. The answer is none. There would be no actionable result from characterizing the devices because the design of the device is already set in stone. So I freed up a lot of time for the important assembly work by pushing the task of characterizing the devices until after the deployment of the devices. This adheres to the principle of least action.

 

In both cases of the principle of least action and the David Bowman technique, it is useful to constantly question every action one makes. Again, this extends the time needed to complete tasks, but it allows for better application of the principle of least action and the David Bowman technique, which will save you time and money. Their benefits will scale with the complexity of the project and compound with the duration.
